Articles about education

  • Education versus college entrance requirements - William B. Curry 1931

    • I found this article to resonate with the current status of secondary education. This article is rather short and goes on to essentially condemn how college entrance requirements at the time dictate high school curriculum with deleterious effects. For example, it places restrictions on a student's ability to take deep dives on particular subject matters that they find intrinsically pulled towards because they must dedicate time elsewhere to meet college requirements. From brief introspection, I find this to be somewhat the case these days. I spent a lot of time in high school preparing for college entrance exams or state wide exams and in classes that I didn't really care about (english and some social studies). If you were to play devil's advocate, then you would say that 'kids shouldn't just do what they want because if they did then they would just eat candy all day long etc'. Which I agree, I think there is a certain level of material that everyone should be exposed to. It is only the exact amount of time which I had a problem. For example, I took an english class every year in high school. This class met 5 days a week, for approximately 1 hour, for the entire school year. My school went from September to June (lets say 8 months of school). That would mean I was in english class for 8 months * 4 weeks/month * 5days/week * 1 hr/day = 160 hours of english per school year. I just wonder how effectively that time was being used on me. (also this isn't a dis on english, I think its a useful topic but most of it didn't interest me personally. Somebody could have ~wasted a similar amount of time in physics when they were more personally interested in english.)
    Furthermore, the subject matter in many of my classes were so broad that it felt like most of the content I learned was surface value memorization of facts and formulas as opposed to learning how these ideas had been generated and how they could be related to things in the modern world around me. Furthermore, I never even felt as if I questioned anything I was learning in high school. I just memorized things I was told and completed assignments as instructed. However, maybe I'm just a hater.

  • Learning to live - Joseph S. Butterweck 1933

    • I like this very brief article because of the author's analytical method. Essentially, the author asks the question 'Are our schools teaching our children how to live?'. Then to answer this, the author assesses the activity of the average 10th grade student and tries to see how the actual experience relates to living a 'more wholesome, a richer, and more beneficial life?'. This reminds me of a lesson I learned in graduate school by writing a review article. I learned from my mentor, that when writing a review you want every sentence in the article to be related to the title. If the sentence you are writing is not addressing the topic in which you claim to be writing, then it is most likely fluff and to be deleted. It might be a stretch but I think this sort of logic applies to everything you design. You cannot effectively design and engineer something without a purpose. And I think when you take a look at educational practices today and ask the same question, 'what is the point of this?', it feels like the current point of educational practices today are designed to help students check boxes to get into college and then get a job. But I think this is drastically different than what I think the point of education should be.
