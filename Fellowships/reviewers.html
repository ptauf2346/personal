Reviewers (and their comments)

As long as your grant passes some initial checks to get reviewed, it is very typical for you to receive comments or grades back from the actual people who reviewed your content. Now seeing these comments can make you feel some type of way, but the feedback is generally quite useful. Grants are highly competitive but many of them allow you to apply more than once. Thus it can be very helpful to go through your reviewer comments very seriously to look for definite areas to improve on your next application. In fact in some applications, there is a specific prompt for you to complete if it is a resubmission and you must make your case showing where you improved from the previous application.

    Some common review metrics:
  • Impact of research
  • Personal evaluation (your academic/research background)
  • Training plan evaluation (is this fellowship going to make you a better scientist)
  • Research proposal evaluation (is your research sound)
  • Personal statement (are you unique and driven)
  • Letters of recommendation (how much do people like you?)
  • Alignment with program goals (do you personally fit the mission statement for the fellowship)

Easy improvements

  • Clarity: I can get annoyed seeing comments where the reviewer is asking something that is clearly in the research proposal. However, if it isn't visible or obvious to the reviewer, than it is 100% your fault. When writing these applications, you have to remember that the entire burden of proof is on you to convince the reviewers that you are worthy of this fellowship/grant. So I try to take these comments in stride as they are showing me my own weaknesses in communication and will help me be a better writer down the line.
  • Methods: It's impossible to know everything, and often reviewers are experts in a niche field that is tangentially related to your research and will be partially biased (in a good way) in what types of experiments they are looking for. It can be good to consider the additional experiments that the reviewer suggests as it could strengthen your proposal and you aren't necessarily tied down to performing the exact experiments you propose. Furthermore these comments can just highlight areas you are technically weak on, but soon to be stronger.
  • Weak impact: Often this can just be fixed by relating your work to the bigger picture. You may think your research is really cool and important, but again the burden of proof is on you to convince the reviewers without a doubt that this research is of paramount importance to the field or the public or etc.
